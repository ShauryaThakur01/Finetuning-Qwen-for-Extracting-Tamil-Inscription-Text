\begin{abstract}
Ancient stone inscriptions written in scripts such as Tamili and Vattezhuthu are an important source of historical and linguistic knowledge. However, reading and interpreting these inscriptions is difficult because the text is often damaged, unevenly carved on stone surfaces, and written in scripts that are no longer commonly used. In addition, there are very limited labeled datasets available for training modern machine learning models. This project aims to explore how recent vision–language models can be used to automatically recognize and interpret these inscriptions.
The proposed system combines transformer-based vision language model Qwen 2.5-3B to extract text from images of inscriptions. Because real inscription datasets are limited, synthetic images will be generated by rendering text and placing it on stone-like backgrounds. To ensure realism, we have utilized the “Aksharamukha” python package to convert the inscription text to image and used "Augraphy" library along with standard pre-processing to combine the inscription image to stone background.  After this, qwen 2.5-3B model is then fine-tuned using a parameter-efficient method LoRA on our custom dataset and qwen2.5-0.5B model is used to convert the recognized characters to human recognizable form. 
After recognizing the characters and words, additional language processing methods will be used to reconstruct sentences and convert the text into modern Tamil. The system will be evaluated using BLUE and ROUGE evaluation metrics. The overall goal of this work is to investigate how modern NLP techniques can support the digital preservation and interpretation of historical inscriptions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Methodology}

To overcome the dual challenges of severe epigraphic degradation and continuous-script morphology, we implemented a decoupled, two-stage cascaded architecture. A monolithic approach—forcing a single model to perform both visual extraction and linguistic Sandhi (\textit{Puṇarcchi}) resolution—yields sub-optimal convergence and exceeds our 20GB VRAM hardware constraint. Therefore, we isolated these tasks into specialized Vision and Linguistic experts.

\subsection{Stage 1: Visual Extraction Expert}
We utilized the Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct model \cite{bai2025qwen25vltechnicalreport}, leveraging its native Multimodal Rotary Positional Encoding (mRoPE) \cite{10.1007/978-981-95-4724-1_35} to process uneven stone surface topography without aspect-ratio distortion. Because the base tokenizer lacked extinct Tamili and Vattezhuthu characters, we engineered a vocabulary injection protocol: missing tokens were extracted from our custom tokenizer and safely injected into the 3B model by dynamically resizing the GPU embedding matrix.

To train within the 20GB VRAM limit, we applied 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) QLoRA. LoRA adapters ($r=16, \alpha=32$) targeted the attention heads, while core MLP layers remained frozen to prevent catastrophic forgetting. Crucially, the \texttt{embed\_tokens} and \texttt{lm\_head} layers were explicitly unfrozen to learn the new vocabulary. Utilizing the \texttt{paged\_adamw\_8bit} optimizer and expandable memory segments, we successfully trained the model on a highly stratified subset of 6,000 synthetic image-text pairs (split 80/20), strictly balancing the Tamili and Vattezhuthu classes.

\subsection{Stage 2: Morphological Segmentation Expert}
    The historically accurate, continuous string output from Stage 1 is passed to Qwen2.5-0.5B, an ultra-lightweight causal language model dedicated exclusively to resolving grammatical Sandhi boundaries and inserting modern spacing. To achieve high accuracy with a sub-billion parameter model, we engineered a sliding-window chunking algorithm across our text corpora. By artificially restricting inputs to 15-word micro-prompts, we generated 25,611 highly localized training pairs (1,000 reserved for validation). This constrained context window forces the model's attention mechanism to focus entirely on local syntax rather than hallucinating overarching semantic narratives, resulting in a highly responsive, low-latency deployment.

To test our model, we utilized two evaluation metrics BLUE and ROUGE. BLUE score tells how accurately our model can extract text from a given image while ROUGE score(s) tells how accurately our model is able to recognize each character. We tested our model with our test set which we got during the train-test split step and with some text-images (black \& white) on which the model was trained. When we tested on the black \& white images, we got a BLUE score of 95.40 and a ROUGE score of 0.9881 (ROUGE-1), 0.9657(ROUGE-2) and 0.9838(0.9838) which tells us that the model (qwen 2.5-3B) was trained well on the training set. Next, we tested the model on the test set which consisted of augmented images (with stone background) and achieved a BLEU Score of 63.02 and a ROUGE score of 0.8796(ROUGE-1), 0.7087(ROUGE-2) and 0.8032(ROUGE-L), indicating that the qwen 2.5-3B performs well on extracting text from images. Next, our segmentation model achieved a BLUE Score of 72.02 and ROUGE Score of 0.8471(ROUGE-1), 0.7636(ROUGE-2) and 0.8469(ROUGE-L) which indicates that the qwen 2.5-0.5B paramter model performs well on segmenting the continuous text received from qwen 2.5-3B model and converting it to human readable form.

This project served as an essential way to learn that a model's performance is often a reflection of how well you respect the constraints of data and hardware. Before this project, I viewed finetuning to be a brute force process like you feed model the data and it learns. However, when I encountered unfair tokenization, it taught me a lesson that Architecture is not script-agnostic. No amount of training data is useful if the model’s internal vocabulary (the tokenizer) cannot "see" the characters correctly. Furthermore, the decision to split the task into two models taught me the value of architectural humility. Initially, I thought one single model is enough for the entire methodology but later realized that for better performance we need to have a separate models.
% • How, exactly, did you learn it? Connect your learning to specific details in your
% description and insights from your analysis.
I learned this through the failure of my initial monolithic model assumptions. During the early stage of development, it was revealed that the monolithic model approach was producing hallucinated character sequences. By tracing, it back to the Tokenization Fairness concept from the course, the base model was treating a lot of ancient characters as unknown or fragmented sub-words. This was not just a data problem but it was an architectural misalignment. I learned the necessity of vocabulary injection by manually extracting tokens from a custom tokenizer and dynamically resizing the GPU embedding matrix—a process that forced me to manipulate the model’s "sight" rather than just its "memory." Furthermore, the insight related to Architectural Humility came from our 20GB VRAM hardware wall. When I attempted to handle both OCR and segmentation together, the model's convergence was sub-optimal. This taught me the value of decoupled pipeline for two separate task.This technical maneuver, inspired by the Mixture of Experts theory, proved that intelligence in NLP often comes from structural constraints rather than just raw parameter count.
